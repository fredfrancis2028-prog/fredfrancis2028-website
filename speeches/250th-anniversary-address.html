The 250th Anniversary Address

Delivered July 4, 2026

Video Coming Soon

Two hundred and fifty years ago today, a group of rebels declared our independence.

You will hear many speeches today about what that declaration meant, what it cost, and what it demands of us now.

This is not one of those speeches.

Instead, this is a story about two men who signed that document, who built this country together, and who nearly tore apart the very nation they risked their lives to create.  It is a story of how, older and wiser, they found their way back to each other—and left a map for us to do the same.

It’s a story most of us were never taught.  But it might be the most important history lesson we have.  Because it proves that the path from crisis to common ground is a road this country has walked before.

On July the Fourth, 1826—fifty years to the day after the Declaration was signed—two men died within five hours of each other.  One in Massachusetts.  One in Virginia.  They were our second and third Presidents.  They were architects of the Declaration of Independence whose anniversary we celebrate today, and of the republic it called into being.

We are not here to honor them as presidents.  We are not here to honor them as framers.  We are here because of the argument between them—the argument that shaped our system of government, the crisis that nearly destroyed it, and the reconciliation that proved this nation could survive its own worst impulses.

What John Adams and Thomas Jefferson gave this country was not just a declaration or a constitution.  It was the proof—hard-won, painfully learned—that argument, in America, can both break us and heal us.

And we get to choose which.

We imagine the founding as a dignified affair.  There is a famous painting—you have seen it, it is on the back of the two-dollar bill—that shows the presentation of the Declaration as a scene of calm consensus.  Gentlemen in powdered wigs, standing in quiet agreement.

It was nothing like that.

These were not marble statues.  These were young men, most of them under forty-five, who had just launched a revolution against the most powerful empire on earth—and who knew that the British would hang them if they failed.  They were scared, they were determined, and they disagreed with each other as passionately as they opposed the crown they were rebelling against.

But they understood something we have begun to forget: that when the stakes are high, disagreement in a democracy is not a luxury.  It is a necessity.  You do not build a republic to last by letting a single voice drown out the rest.

And no two voices clashed more fiercely than those of Thomas Jefferson and John Adams.

Jefferson believed that the greatest threat to the new nation was its own government—that power, once concentrated, would grow until it consumed the liberty it was created to protect.  He trusted the people.  He feared the institution.

Adams believed the opposite—that without a strong and stable government, the people could be exploited by opportunists who would use division for their own benefit.  He trusted the institution.  He feared the mob.

They were both right.  And they were both wrong.

But because neither man would yield, the system they built together bears the fingerprints of both.  Jefferson's instinct to limit government power lives in the Bill of Rights, in the freedoms we guard against federal overreach.  Adams's insistence on institutional stability lives in the structure itself—in the checks, the balances, the separation of powers designed to prevent any one faction from seizing control.

The tension between their two visions is not a bug in the American software. It is the feature. It is the blueprint.  It is the argument we are still having, in every election, right up to this day—and it is one we should be having.  The founders knew that honest argument between people of genuine conviction would produce better answers than either side could reach alone.

But that is not what happened next.

Within a decade of the founding, the argument crossed a line.  The young republic divided into fiercely opposed camps—Adams's Federalists and Jefferson's Democratic-Republicans—and both sides stopped believing in the good faith of the other.  No armies marched.  No shots were fired.  The crisis of the 1790s was not a civil war.  It was something quieter and, in its way, more dangerous—the collapse of the basic democratic understanding that your political opponent is still a legitimate participant in the process of governing.

When that belief dies, a democracy does not need a battlefield to begin tearing itself apart.

The partisan press of the 1790s was savage.  Federalist newspapers called Jefferson a godless radical who would bring the chaos and the guillotine of the French Revolution to American soil.  Republican newspapers called Adams a tyrant and a secret monarchist plotting to crown himself king.  These were not fringe voices shouting from the margins.  These were the leading publications of the day, and the language was as vicious, as personal, and as reckless with the truth as anything we encounter today.

It got worse.  Adams signed the Sedition Act of 1798, making it a federal crime to publish criticism of the president or of Congress.  Newspaper editors were arrested.  Publishers were jailed.  The government of the United States used the machinery of law to silence political opposition—and it did so in the name of national security.  The law specifically protected the president from criticism, but not the vice president.  Because in those days, the Vice President was the runner-up in the election.  And that was Jefferson.

Jefferson's response was no less dangerous.  Working in secret, he drafted resolutions arguing that individual states could simply refuse to obey federal law—an argument born of genuine alarm, but one that contained the seeds of secession; the poisonous weed that would grow into our civil war, sixty years later.

Both men had gone too far.  Both men had allowed their fear of the other's vision to override the principles they claimed to defend.  Adams, the champion of institutional stability, had used the institution to crush dissent.  Jefferson, the champion of individual liberty, had proposed a remedy that could dissolve the union itself.

Each had become the thing the other most feared.

So when the election of 1800 was held, it was in an atmosphere of genuine dread.  Both sides believed the republic would not survive if the other side won.  Serious commentators questioned whether the loser would accept the result—whether there could indeed be a peaceful transfer of power at all.

It did happen, just.  On the morning of Jefferson's inauguration, Adams left the capital before dawn.  He could not bring himself to watch the ceremony, but he left.  He transferred power peacefully.  Despite his anger, despite his fear, he was an American first, before he was a Federalist, and the system he believed in demanded no less of him.  Because to do otherwise would have been to forever stain his honor and live the rest of his life a hypocrite.

They did not speak again for more than a decade.

When we look back at that dark chapter, it is easy to blame the primitive tools of their time—the partisan broadsheets, the ink-stained pamphlets, the slow speed of a letter carried on horseback.  We like to think we have evolved past their panic.

But human nature hasn’t changed in two hundred and fifty years.  Only our machinery has.

Today, we have traded the printing press for the algorithm.  We have taken the same fear, the same suspicion, and the same impulse to silence our opponents, and we have accelerated it to the speed of light.

Online, we have built digital trenches.  We fire words across them—not to persuade, but to destroy anyone who dares disagree with us.  We have begun to treat one another precisely as that British King treated our whole nation: as subjects to be ruled, rather than as countrymen about whom we care.

The remedy is not less speech.  It is more speech, and better listening, on all sides.  A grievance is not a declaration of war.  An idea we disagree with is not a threat to our existence.  Sitting together around our national table, it is a request to be heard—and an invitation to re-examine our own ideas in a new light.

What they asked of each other—and what they ask of us—is hard.

I know.  Because I have failed at it myself.

I have felt that dangerous, seductive pull to write off the other side.  I have chosen anger when I should have chosen patience.  I have spoken when I should have listened.  And every single time I stopped listening, I stopped learning.  Every time, I risked missing a piece of the truth that only the other person could see.

If an issue is worth getting angry about, it is an issue worth getting right. And you cannot get it right if you are only talking to an echo chamber.

But I have also seen what happens when we do the harder, better thing.  When we grant someone we disagree with what Adams and Jefferson finally granted each other: the presumption that they are not our enemy, but a fellow citizen who loves this country as fiercely as we do, and who may see something we have missed.  I have seen it change the conversation.  I have seen it change the people in the conversation.

I've been lucky enough to have it change me.

In the middle of all their pain, Adams and Jefferson finally picked up their pens and began to write to each other again.

Benjamin Rush, a fellow signer of the Declaration, believed the republic needed their friendship as much as it had needed their argument.  He saw their estrangement as the nation's most visible wound—two founders teaching the country, by their silence, that political differences were beyond repair.

He refused to accept that lesson.

Through his patient intervention, they began to write.  Cautiously at first, then with increasing good will, and finally, with the tenderness of old soldiers who remember why they once fought side by side.

Those letters survive.  More than a hundred and fifty of them, preserved in their own handwriting—a record of two stubborn men teaching themselves how to listen again.  Letter by letter, they rebuilt what anger over politics had destroyed.  Not by agreeing—they never changed their perspectives—but by recognizing something more important than agreement: that the person on the other side of the argument was not an enemy.  He was an adversary.

And those words are not synonyms.

An enemy is someone you seek to destroy.  An adversary is someone who makes you stronger as you struggle with one another, testing you, challenging your ideas.  An enemy must be silenced.  An adversary must be heard—because the contest between your ideas and theirs is the very mechanism by which free people find their way.

Adams and Jefferson learned this.  It took them decades, but they learned.

When they died in 1826—on this day, two hundred years ago, on the fiftieth birthday of the nation they had argued into existence—John Adams's last words were: "Thomas Jefferson survives."

On his deathbed, what comforted John Adams was not his own legacy, not his own place in history—it was the belief that his old adversary was still alive.  That the man he had fought with for thirty years, his worthy adversary, was still out there.  Still part of their argument.

Adams was wrong about the fact; Jefferson had died that morning, five hours earlier.  But he was right about their shared, fundamental truth: that you can be a fierce adversary without becoming a mortal enemy.  That is the compact at the heart of this republic.  That is the enduring legacy they gave to this country, together—not just in the documents they signed, but in the letters they wrote when they had nothing left to prove to anyone but each other.

We stand at our two-hundred-and-fiftieth anniversary, and the question before us is the same one they answered with their last breath:

Is what we built together, all those years ago, still worth the argument?

I believe it is.  And we prove it to our neighbors not with words alone, but with the daily act of treating our adversaries as neighbors, whose piece of the truth we have not yet heard, rather than enemies to be destroyed.

Adams and Jefferson showed us how.  Their letters showed us the way.  Now it is our turn.

Let us begin.
