Reclaiming the Unfinished Promise: A Call to Action

Video Coming Soon

A Speech by Fred H. Francis

I. The Pain of the Moment

My friends, we are living through a season of deep frustration. When we look at recent decisions from our highest courts, when we witness the systematic dismantling of voting rights that generations of Americans bled to secure, it is entirely understandable to feel a sense of profound dismay.

Just recently, a community leader on Nantucket made the difficult decision to cancel their annual public reading of the Declaration of Independence. I recognized the pain behind that decision immediately. It came from a place of honest moral outrage—a refusal to celebrate a promise that, for too many Americans, remains unfulfilled.

The historical fact is undeniable: the rights and privileges enshrined in our founding documents have tragically, often violently, and unequally been applied. Anyone who denies that is either not paying attention or not being honest.

But today, I want to pose a critical question to our party and our country: How do we respond to this pain?

II. The Strategic Mistake of Retreat

I believe with everything in me that walking away from our founding words is a profound strategic mistake. Canceling the reading of the Declaration of Independence is not a protest. It is a concession. It concedes exactly what those who would diminish American democracy most need us to concede: that our founding documents belong only to them.

But those words do not belong to them. They belong to us. And the gravity of this moment is precisely the measure of how urgently we must reclaim them.

The Founders did not build some perfect crystal monument for us to passively admire. They knew they didn't. The system they built was manifestly imperfect—a system that permitted chattel slavery, excluded women from the franchise, and restricted citizenship to wealthy men of property. They knew the weight of these compromises.

Yet, they chose an imperfect union over a principled defeat. Why? Because they understood that a fractured coalition would be destroyed piecemeal. More importantly, they built into that very union the exact mechanisms—the amendment process, the separation of powers, an independent judiciary—to correct the sins they could not then abolish.

III. The Tradition of Resistance

The value of the American project has never been in its initial conditions. It has always been in its capacity for self-correction.

When Thomas Jefferson wrote that "all men are created equal," he was not describing the world as it existed in 1776. He was declaring the world as it ought to be—a standard gauge against which every subsequent generation would measure itself.

We are not the first generation to face this gap between promise and reality.

  • In 1852, Frederick Douglass stood before an audience on the day after the Fourth of July and delivered a blistering indictment of American hypocrisy. But Douglass did not reject the Constitution. He claimed it. He called it a "glorious liberty document" and insisted that the slaveholders, not the abolitionists, were the ones betraying it.
  • A century later, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. stood before the Lincoln Memorial and did the exact same thing. He did not call the founding promises a lie to be discarded; he called them a promissory note—a debt to be collected.

Douglass did not cancel the reading. He read it louder. He turned the founding words into weapons against the injustice they had been used to conceal. That is the tradition we inherit. The act of reading these words in times of crisis is not a celebration of a completed achievement; it is an act of resistance.

IV. An Ethics of Obligation

I was raised in a minister’s household. My father studied philosophy and theology during the height of the Civil Rights Movement, under the shadow of the Freedom Rides. I grew up on the moral reasoning of thinkers like Reinhold Niebuhr, who taught a timeless truth: Our capacity for justice makes democracy possible, but our persistent inclination toward injustice makes democracy necessary.

Right now, we are seeing that inclination toward injustice. But our collective outrage is evidence of our capacity for justice.

We bear an inescapable, binding obligation to one another. I derive this from the Parable of the Good Samaritan. The duty to help the traveler on the road to Jericho was not suspended by the danger of the road. It was activated by it.

Our duty to our fellow citizens does not evaporate when the political landscape gets harder. It intensifies. The answer to an assault on American democracy is not to withdraw from the instruments of democracy—it is to use them with greater skill, greater clarity, and greater moral conviction than ever before.

V. The Path to 2028

As I look toward the presidency in 2028, this is the conviction that drives me. I am not running to preserve a status quo, nor am I running to mourn what has been lost. I am running to climb the highest steeple I can find and articulate the values that can heal and rebuild this nation.

We must turn no one away. We must not withdraw. We must reach out even to those with whom we fiercely disagree, recognizing that the Americans who are most anguished right now are precisely the ones whose conscience and stubborn refusal to give up are most desperately needed.

The tools of self-correction are still in our hands. The only question that matters now is whether we will allow our legitimate dismay to blind us, or whether we will take up those tools and keep building.

Let us read the Declaration. Let us read the Bill of Rights beside it. And let us tell the world what those words demand of us today: not surrender, but an unwavering, fierce insistence on fulfilling the unfinished promise of America.

Thank you, and God bless you.
