We’re just getting started.

This campaign launched with the belief that you earn endorsements by standing for something worth endorsing.  We’re working on that.  If you or your organization would like to be among the first, we’d be honored to hear from you.

Just as We Endorse reflects the living allies whose work shapes this campaign, this tab is a place to pay tribute to the people, organizations, and moments no longer with us whose example still does.  A person, an institution, an event from the past — each honored here for the same reason: because remembering them honestly is part of how we decide who to be now.

The March on Washington (1963)

More than 250,000 Americans gathered at the Lincoln Memorial to demand jobs and freedom, where Dr. King delivered “I Have a Dream.”  It remains living proof that disciplined, peaceful protest can move a nation’s conscience.

Franklin D. Roosevelt

Led the nation through the Great Depression and the Second World War, and built the modern framework of American economic security without ever losing sight of who government is supposed to serve.

Eleanor Roosevelt

First Lady, delegate to the United Nations, and principal author of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights — she insisted that rights meant nothing unless they reached everyone, and spent a lifetime making sure more people were included in “everyone.”

Shirley Chisholm

The first Black woman elected to Congress, and the first to seek a major party’s presidential nomination.  “Unbought and unbossed,” she called herself, and she meant it.

Bella Abzug

A fierce voice in Congress for women’s rights, civil rights, and government accountability, known for holding her ground as firmly as she held her opinions.

Barbara Jordan

The first Black woman elected to Congress from the Deep South.  During the Watergate hearings she declared, “My faith in the Constitution is whole, it is complete, it is total” — one of the clearest statements of constitutional conscience ever spoken on the record.

Tip O’Neill

Speaker of the House for a decade, who governed by the conviction that a Democrat and a Republican could argue fiercely by day and remain friends by evening, without ever mistaking the other for an enemy.

Colin Powell

Soldier, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, and Secretary of State, who served presidents of both parties and held himself to the same standard of candor regardless of who was asking.

Martin Luther King Jr.

Called this country to live up to its own founding words, not abandon them — insisting to the end that the Declaration and the Constitution were promises still owed, not lies to be discarded.

John F. Kennedy

Asked what you could do for your country, and treated it as a governing philosophy rather than a slogan.

Robert F. Kennedy

Carried his brother’s unfinished work forward, and on the night of Dr. King’s assassination, asked a grieving crowd in Indianapolis to answer violence with compassion instead of vengeance — and that night, they did.

Rev. James Lawson

Trained a generation of young activists in the discipline of nonviolent resistance, giving the Civil Rights Movement not just its moral clarity but its method.

Rev. William Sloane Coffin

Freedom Rider and campus chaplain who believed a faith without public consequence wasn’t much of a faith at all.

Omar Bradley

The “soldier’s general,” who led American forces from North Africa to Germany and never lost the trust of the men under his command.

George S. Patton

A relentless, uncompromising battlefield commander whose Third Army’s drive across Europe helped bring the war in the West to its close.

Deke Slayton

One of the original Mercury Seven astronauts, grounded for over a decade by a heart condition, who never stopped working toward space until he finally flew, on the Apollo-Soyuz mission in 1975.

Gene Kranz

Flight Director for Apollo 13, whose “failure is not an option” wasn’t a line written for a movie — it was how Mission Control actually operated, and it brought three astronauts home.

Chris Kraft

NASA’s first Flight Director, who built the discipline and culture of Mission Control from nothing, and whose standards still govern how the agency operates today.

Abraham Lincoln

Held a divided union together through its worst hour, and left behind the clearest sentence ever written about what self-government is for: government “of the people, by the people, for the people.”

George C. Marshall

Army Chief of Staff during the Second World War and architect of the Marshall Plan, who understood that winning a war and winning the peace after it are two different achievements, requiring two different kinds of resolve.

George Low

NASA’s Apollo Spacecraft Program Manager, who pushed for and won approval to send Apollo 8 all the way to lunar orbit a full mission ahead of schedule — a decision historians still call one of the boldest in the history of American spaceflight.

Diogenes the Cynic

Also known as Diogenes of Sinope, the ancient Greek philosopher said to have wandered Athens by daylight carrying a lit lamp, searching for “an honest man.”  Long before “cynic” came to mean something small and sour, Diogenes used it to mean something else entirely: the refusal to accept comfortable lies.
